The government is bulldozing greenbelt land. It is overriding local planning objections. It is forcing hundreds of new homes onto villages that don't want them, rewriting planning rules to take farmland, and building entire new settlements against the wishes of the people who already live nearby. It says it has no choice — Britain needs 1.5 million new homes, 300,000 every year, and the land has to come from somewhere.

That is a lie by omission. The numbers that follow come not from Reform UK, not from the Daily Mail, and not from political opinion. They come from the Home Office, the Office for National Statistics, and the Department for Work and Pensions. They are government figures. And they tell a story this government would rather you didn't read.

Part I

Who Is Actually Here

There are currently 6.5 million non-British citizens living in the United Kingdom. That figure comes from the Migration Observatory and the House of Commons Library, drawing on ONS population estimates for late 2025 and early 2026.

Of those 6.5 million, approximately one million are on genuine skilled worker visas — doctors, nurses, engineers, programmers, the people running businesses and filling roles that would otherwise go empty. Nobody is suggesting those people leave. They are here legally, they are working, they are paying tax, and under any rational policy they stay.

That leaves 5.5 million non-citizens who are not in skilled employment. They live in houses. Their children take school places. They attend GP surgeries and A&E departments. Some have been here for years, drawing benefits, speaking no English, living in enclaves of their own nationality, contributing nothing to the country that is housing and feeding them.

Part II

The Benefit Dependency Numbers

In July 2025 the DWP published, for the first time, a breakdown of Universal Credit claimants by immigration status. The figures were stark. Of the 8 million people on Universal Credit, approximately 1.27 million were non-British or non-Irish nationals — one in six of all claimants.

That number had risen 40 percent in just three years — from 906,000 in June 2022 to 1.27 million in June 2025. At the same time, the government was publicly claiming to be tightening immigration rules.

Of those 1.27 million non-citizen UC claimants, 737,799 are fully unemployed. Not low-paid workers receiving a top-up. Unemployed. Every single one of them has been legally resident in the UK for a minimum of five years — because that is the legal requirement to claim Universal Credit as a non-citizen. Five years minimum. Still not working.

Among refugees on UC the picture is even starker. Only 22 percent are in work. That means 78 percent — roughly 136,000 people — are on benefits having been granted protection in this country, with no apparent intention or requirement to contribute to it.

Part III

What Removal Would Free Up

Using Home Office occupancy figures and ONS household size data, the scale of housing relief from targeted removal becomes clear. Start with the most immediate cost.

At the end of December 2025, 107,003 people were in Home Office asylum accommodation. Of these, 30,657 were in hotels — at a cost running to over a billion pounds a month when benefits are included — and 72,769 were in dispersal housing, properties spread across the country that are unavailable to the local rental market.

At average occupancy rates for this group — approximately 2.7 people per household, blending single adults in hotels with families in dispersal housing — that 107,000 people represents roughly 38,000 to 40,000 housing units. Freed immediately. Hotel rooms returned to the tourism economy. Dispersal properties returned to local rental markets.

Then the longer-term picture. Targeting only the long-term unemployed non-citizen UC claimants — conservatively half to two-thirds of the 1.27 million, approximately 600,000 to 900,000 people — frees up a further 220,000 to 330,000 households, calculated at ONS household size averages for this demographic.

Combined total: 260,000 to 370,000 homes freed. Without building a single one.

The greenbelt destruction, the village expansions nobody voted for, the farmland being concreted over — none of it would be necessary if the government dealt honestly with who is occupying the housing stock.

Part IV

The NHS Red Herring

Every time this subject is raised, someone mentions the NHS. We cannot remove non-citizens because the NHS depends on them. This is a deliberate confusion of two entirely separate groups of people.

There are approximately 180,000 to 220,000 non-citizen NHS workers in the UK. Every single one of them is on a skilled worker visa. Every single one is barred by the conditions of that visa from claiming Universal Credit. The overlap between NHS foreign workers and long-term unemployed non-citizen benefit claimants is effectively zero. They are not the same people. Presenting them as the same risk is either ignorance or deliberate dishonesty.

Those NHS workers stay. That is not a concession — it is simply what the policy already says. The BAME figure of 47.5 percent, which is frequently cited in this debate, includes British-born people of ethnic minority backgrounds who have nothing whatsoever to do with immigration status. Its inclusion in this argument is not a mistake. It is misdirection.

Part V

The Skilled Visa Con

There is a separate question about what actually qualifies as a skilled worker visa — and it deserves its own article. But briefly: when the government raised the skilled worker salary threshold to £41,700, applications in food preparation and hospitality collapsed by 81 percent. Care worker visa applications collapsed by 91 percent.

Genuinely skilled workers already earn above that threshold. The collapse proved these were low-wage, low-skill jobs being laundered through a skilled visa category to get round immigration controls. Between 250,000 and 300,000 people are currently in the UK on Health and Care Worker visas doing care home and home care work — jobs with no mandatory qualifications, repeatedly flagged by the Care Quality Commission for poor standards. That is not skilled migration. That is cheap labour in a visa costume.

Then there are the back door routes. Charity worker visas require no qualifications. Religious worker visas have been abused repeatedly — multiple prosecutions have involved fake religious institutions set up purely as visa sponsors. Creative worker visas have been used to bring in performers for venues that turned out to be fronts. None of these withstand scrutiny as genuine skilled or necessary migration.

The proof is the salary threshold test. When the bar was raised, these routes collapsed. Genuine skill commands a genuine salary. The collapse told us everything.

Part VI

The Annual Flow Problem

Dealing with the existing stock is one half of the equation. The other half is what keeps arriving each year and adding to demand.

In 2025, setting aside seasonal agricultural workers and the Youth Mobility Scheme — which is a broadly reciprocal arrangement — approximately 64,000 people entered the UK through channels that serve no clear national interest: 46,000 detected illegal and irregular arrivals via small boats and clandestine methods, plus 18,000 on charity, religious and creative worker visas.

Add the 54,887 asylum grants per year that move into mainstream housing and the NHS and school system, and you are generating between 85,000 and 90,000 households of entirely avoidable new housing demand every single year. That is getting on for one new home in every three the government is currently building, needed solely because of flows a competent government would stop.

The UK's natural population growth — births minus deaths among the settled British population — is currently close to flat. Without migration-driven population growth, new housing demand from demographic change alone would be minimal. The housing crisis is not a British birth rate problem. It is a population growth problem driven almost entirely by migration, and the government is responding to it by destroying the British countryside rather than addressing the cause.

Part VII

Reform UK's Plan

Reform UK's Operation Restoring Justice, published August 2025, sets out a five-year programme to identify, detain and deport illegal migrants. The illegal migrant population alone — those with no lawful right to remain — is estimated at over one million people. The state currently spends over £7 billion a year housing and supporting them. Reform's figures project a net saving of £7 billion in the first parliamentary term and £42 billion over a decade.

The numbers in this article go beyond illegal migrants alone. They include the legal non-citizens who have been here for years, taken what the British taxpayer provides, and given nothing back. The question is not whether Britain is a welcoming country. It is. The question is whether welcome has an end point — and whether a government that builds on greenbelt land rather than asking that question is serving the people who already live here.

The maths is not complicated. The will to do it is the only thing that has ever been missing.
