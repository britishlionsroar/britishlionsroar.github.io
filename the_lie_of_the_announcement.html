The Lie of the Announcement

Yesterday the Home Secretary announced police would stop policing your tweets. The same government that made the announcement has built the most comprehensive architecture of speech suppression in British peacetime history. Here is what they did not tell you.
Steve English — The British Lions Roar 1 April 2026
250,000 NCHIs on police databases since 2014 — untouched by yesterday's announcement
137,550 Hate crimes recorded in England and Wales, year ending March 2025
30+ Arrests every single day for online communications offences — continuing under Labour

The announcement was well-crafted. Home Secretary Shabana Mahmood declared that police would no longer investigate online spats. Officers would stop policing legal tweets. Forces would return to "patrolling our streets, catching criminals, and keeping communities safe." The Free Speech Union welcomed it. The Telegraph led on it. By mid-morning it was being shared as a victory.

It was not a victory. It was a press release about future recording thresholds. Every word of it was technically accurate. Almost none of it was honest.

The database they didn't mention

Since the College of Policing introduced operational guidance for Non-Crime Hate Incidents in 2014, approximately 250,000 have been recorded on police databases across England and Wales. That figure comes from official statements — confirmed by Mims Davies MP and the government's own response to the final NCHI report published yesterday alongside the announcement.

Yesterday's reforms address none of them.

Not one record will be deleted. Not one person will be notified that their entry exists. Not one DBS check will be cleared. Every teacher, healthcare worker, care home employee, police officer, or volunteer who appears on an enhanced Disclosure and Barring Service check carrying a non-crime hate entry remains exactly where they were on Tuesday evening. Their career prospects have not changed. Their permanent mark for something that was never a crime has not been reviewed, challenged, or removed.

The Home Secretary raised the threshold for future recordings. She said nothing about the past. A quarter of a million people were not mentioned.

The tsar they did mention — elsewhere

Three weeks ago, on 9 March 2026, Communities Secretary Steve Reed announced the creation of a new "special representative on anti-Muslim hostility" — an Anti-Muslim Hatred Tsar, appointed by government, with a mandate to embed a new official definition of anti-Muslim hostility across employers, schools, universities, the NHS, broadcasters, and local councils.

This was not a minor administrative appointment. The government's own counter-terrorism commissioner, Jonathan Hall, warned it would stifle debate and could give Islamist extremists grounds to silence criticism of their ideology. The Free Speech Union is mounting a legal challenge. Lord Young called it their biggest fight yet, warning that tens of thousands of people a year could lose their jobs at the say-so of a Labour-appointed official.

The definition covers "prejudicial stereotyping" — elastic language that institutions will interpret in the most risk-averse manner available, exactly as they did with NCHIs. The mechanism is identical. The brand is different. A non-statutory definition adopted by public bodies becomes policy, then training, then procedure, then investigation, then sanction — without Parliament ever voting for a blasphemy law.

The Prime Minister gave explicit assurances in December 2025 that there would be no blasphemy law by statute, judicial overreach, or non-statutory definition. The tsar and definition were announced ten weeks later.

"If we don't win this fight, tens of thousands of people a year could lose their jobs at the say-so of a Labour-appointed tsar. It's dystopian."

— Lord Young, Free Speech Union, March 2026

The pub law they passed quietly

The Employment Rights Act 2025, now enacted law, reintroduced employer liability for third-party harassment of staff under Clause 20. Third parties include customers. Harassment includes remarks that staff find offensive — including overheard conversations the member of staff was not part of. Reasonable steps to prevent it are not defined.

The Equality and Human Rights Commission warned Parliament explicitly that this provision would "disproportionately curtail" freedom of expression in overheard conversations, particularly where the speech involved protected religious or philosophical beliefs. Expressing a view about transgender rights, immigration, or religion in a pub could distress a member of staff, who could report it to their manager, who now has a legal incentive to have the customer removed or risk employer liability.

The law does not ban speech. It makes the person who permits it liable. Landlords were handed a choice: police conversation, or accept risk. The last genuinely unmonitored civic spaces in Britain — the public house, the working men's club, the local bar — have been placed under a legal framework that makes free political conversation a commercial hazard.

The monitoring unit they announced to Parliament

In July 2025, Policing Minister Dame Diana Johnson confirmed in a written ministerial statement to MPs the creation of the National Internet Intelligence Investigations team, coordinated by the National Police Coordination Centre. Its stated mandate: to monitor social media intelligence, advise on its use to inform local operational decision-making, and help forces identify public safety threats in real time.

The unit's specific focus areas, as reported to the Home Affairs Select Committee, include anti-migrant posts, "polarising" issues, and "harmful" extremist content. Every police force already maintains a dedicated social media monitoring team. The new unit provides national coordination above them.

The Government's own privacy notice for its social and digital media analysis confirms it collects posts receiving high engagement — including posts that "express political opinions or philosophical beliefs." It states it does not "actively monitor individuals." It does not explain how it collects posts expressing political opinions without monitoring the individuals who expressed them.

Officers are now making more than 30 arrests per day for online communications offences — over 12,000 in 2023 alone, a 58% increase from pre-pandemic levels. Convictions fell by nearly half over the same period. The arrest rate rose. The conviction rate fell. The arrest itself is the punishment.

The aggregation power they wrote into law

Under CPS Social Media Guidelines, investigators are authorised to conduct "pattern of life" analysis — building a case from habits, connections, and timelines across multiple posts. CPS Communications Offences Guidelines instruct prosecutors to look for a "pattern of frequent or large number of concerning communications" to distinguish criminality from isolated incidents. The case of R v CB and Mohammed [2020] EWCA Crim 790 established judicial endorsement for reviewing digital material to demonstrate a pattern of behaviour rather than assessing posts in isolation.

The practical consequence is this: no single post you have ever published needs to be illegal. Investigators can select posts from across your entire online history, aggregate them, and present the pattern as the offence. Your article on halal. Your post on Islamic immigration. Your comment on biological sex. Your substack entry on grooming gangs. Individually lawful. Collectively, in the hands of a prosecutor building a pattern, potentially something else entirely — depending on who is reading, what they are looking for, and which community they decide was caused upset.

The verdict

Hold all of it together and a picture emerges that no single announcement can obscure.

This government raised the recording threshold for NCHIs while leaving 250,000 existing records untouched. It announced police would stop policing tweets while funding a national unit specifically to monitor social media. It said free speech would be protected while enacting a law that makes overheard pub conversations a legal liability for landlords. It promised no blasphemy law while appointing an official whose entire purpose is to embed a speech-restricting definition across every major institution in public life. It told Parliament arrests for online speech were a concern while those arrests continued at 30 a day.

Yesterday's announcement was not a reform. It was a rebranding of one visible and politically embarrassing component of a much larger system — timed for maximum coverage, stripped of context, and designed to be celebrated by people who will not read the rest of the story.

The machinery has not been dismantled. It has been modernised, expanded, and moved somewhere harder to photograph.
