Counter-Terrorism Series — Prevent Programme Failures

The Programme That Cannot Prevent

Misconceived at birth, hollowed out by political cowardice, and confirmed as a failure by its own body count. The case against Prevent — in its own words, from its own record.
  • 8,778 referrals to Prevent in 2024/25 — a record high, up 27% in a single year — yet three girls were murdered in Southport by a boy referred three times and cleared each time
  • 80–90% of all referrals are dismissed at initial assessment; only 17–20% ever reach the Channel programme for serious intervention
  • The Shawcross Independent Review found that "all too often those who commit terrorist acts in this country have been previously referred to Prevent"
  • Nine documented cases — confirmed by official learning reviews, inquests, sentencing remarks, and police statements — where individuals known to Prevent went on to kill, maim, or hold hostages
  • At least three official inquests have found deaths were "probably avoidable" or "should have been prevented" following Prevent failures
  • Prevent was not conceived to counter Islamist terrorism. It was conceived to avoid naming Islamist terrorism — and the bodies are the proof of that distinction
Purpose / Mission This article is part of The British Lions Roar's ongoing counter-terrorism accountability series. It examines the Prevent programme not through the government's promotional framing but through its own official failure record — learning reviews, inquest findings, public inquiry reports, and sentencing remarks. The programme's defenders say it saves lives. What cannot be denied is that it has also, repeatedly, failed to save them.

There is a question the government will not directly answer. How many people referred to the Prevent counter-terrorism programme have gone on to commit terrorist attacks or atrocities? No official aggregate statistic is published. The Home Office's annual Prevent statistics track referral volumes, demographics, and adoption rates. They do not track outcomes. They do not count the dead.

This article does.

The Prevent programme was established in 2003 under Tony Blair's government, in the aftermath of 9/11, as part of the CONTEST counter-terrorism strategy. It became a statutory duty for public bodies under the Counter-Terrorism and Security Act 2015. Its Channel strand offers structured intervention for individuals assessed as vulnerable to radicalisation. On paper, it is the early-warning system between a vulnerable individual and a terrorist attack. On the record — the actual, documented, published record — it is something else.

8,778
Referrals to Prevent in the year ending March 2025 — a record high, up 27% from 6,922 the previous year
Source: Home Office — Individuals Referred to and Supported Through the Prevent Programme, April 2024 to March 2025

Of those 8,778 referrals, roughly 80 to 90 per cent will be dismissed at initial assessment. Only 17 to 20 per cent will be adopted as formal cases into the Channel programme for intensive support. Since 2015, more than 58,000 people have been referred. Over 90 per cent had no counter-terrorism concerns identified. The programme processes an enormous volume of referrals, generates substantial institutional activity, and absorbs substantial public money. What it has not reliably done is prevent terrorists from acting.

The William Shawcross Independent Review of Prevent, published in February 2023, was blunt. It found that Prevent had shown "cultural timidity and an institutional hesitancy to tackle Islamism for fear of charges of Islamophobia." It found that Prevent "too often bestows a status of victimhood on all who come into contact with it." It noted that 80 per cent of counter-terrorism police live investigations were Islamist in nature — yet only 16 per cent of Prevent referrals in 2021-22 were Islamist. And it stated, without equivocation, that "all too often those who commit terrorist acts in this country have been previously referred to Prevent."

"All too often those who commit terrorist acts in this country have been previously referred to Prevent. It apparently failed to understand the danger in these cases."

William Shawcross — Independent Review of Prevent, February 2023

That is not a critic of Prevent speaking. That is the government's own commissioned reviewer, granted access to classified material, assessing the programme on its own terms. What follows is the case record he was referring to — expanded, updated, and documented in full.

The Case Record: Known to Prevent. Went On to Act.

The following nine cases are confirmed through official sources: Prevent learning reviews published on GOV.UK, inquest findings and Prevention of Future Deaths reports from the judiciary, sentencing remarks, Hansard, Metropolitan Police statements, and verified news reporting. Each represents an individual who passed through the Prevent system and was either cleared, closed, or assessed as no further concern — and then acted.

Case 1 — Parsons Green, London — 15 September 2017

Ahmed Hassan — Tube Bomb. 30 Injured.

Iraqi asylum seeker Age 18 Active Channel participant
Who He Was

Ahmed Hassan arrived in the UK in October 2015, claiming asylum. He told Home Office immigration officials in January 2016 that he had been trained by Islamic State — a disclosure that was apparently not passed to counter-terrorism authorities. He was placed in foster care with an elderly couple in Sunbury-on-Thames who were given no information about his IS links. He was enrolled in the Channel programme for over a year, receiving mentoring and support.

The Attack

On 15 September 2017, Hassan planted a homemade TATP bucket bomb on a District line train at Parsons Green Underground station. The device partially detonated. Thirty people were injured, mostly with burns. A full detonation would have caused mass casualties in a packed rush-hour train.

Outcome

Arrested the following day at Dover. Convicted of attempted murder and causing an explosion likely to endanger life. Sentenced to life, minimum 34 years.

Prevent learning review conducted. Channel management failings identified. The IS training disclosure made at immigration interview had not been shared with counter-terrorism authorities.
Case 2 — Fishmongers' Hall, London — 29 November 2019

Usman Khan — Two Killed at Prisoner Rehabilitation Conference.

British-Pakistani Age 28 Prevent-managed on DDP
Who He Was

Usman Khan was convicted in 2012 as part of an al-Qaeda-inspired plot to bomb high-profile London locations and build a terrorist training camp in Pakistan. Released on licence in December 2018. Placed on the Home Office Desistance and Disengagement Programme — a Prevent-linked rehabilitation scheme. Prevent officers from Staffordshire Police were actively involved in his supervision. In June 2019 they took him to a Learning Together event at HMP Whitemoor.

The Attack

Khan attended the Learning Together prisoner rehabilitation conference at Fishmongers' Hall concealing knives and wearing a fake suicide vest. He stabbed five people. Jack Merritt, 25, and Saskia Jones, 23 — both Cambridge graduates who had worked on the very rehabilitation programme Khan was attending — were killed. He was shot dead by police on London Bridge.

Outcome

Killed at the scene by armed officers.

Fishmongers' Hall Inquest Prevention of Future Deaths report (2021): gaps in Prevent officer training; no MAPPA panel had expressly approved Khan's attendance at the conference where he killed.
Case 3 — Streatham High Road, London — 2 February 2020

Sudesh Amman — Stabbing Rampage. Attack May Have Been Prevented.

British-Sri Lankan Age 20 Active surveillance / Prevent pathways
Who He Was

Convicted in 2018 for 16 counts of disseminating terrorist material — including sharing an al-Qaeda magazine in a family WhatsApp group. Sentenced to 3 years 4 months. Automatically released halfway through in January 2020. Placed under active counter-terrorism surveillance post-release, described by The Guardian as "well known to the counter-terror authorities." Involved in Prevent-linked pathways including a mentor. Three days before the attack, undercover officers watched him buy items consistent with making a fake suicide belt — Irn Bru bottles, parcel tape, tin foil. The probation service decided not to recall him to prison.

The Attack

Amman walked to Streatham High Road, stole a knife from a shop, and launched a 62-second stabbing rampage. He stabbed two members of the public before being shot dead by the undercover surveillance officers who had been following him.

Outcome

Shot dead at the scene.

Inquest jury (2021): the attack "may have been prevented" had Amman been recalled to prison after buying the suicide belt components. He was not recalled. He killed two days later.
Case 4 — Forbury Gardens, Reading — 20 June 2020

Khairi Saadallah — Three Killed. Four Prevent Referrals. Deaths "Probably Avoidable."

Libyan asylum seeker Age 25 Four Prevent referrals — all closed
Who He Was

Arrived in the UK as an asylum seeker in 2012. Convicted six times for 15 offences between 2015 and 2019, including eight violent offences and two involving knives. Had served time in prison alongside Abu Izzadeen of the proscribed group al-Muhajiroun. MI5 had intelligence in 2019 about his interest in travelling for extremist purposes. Could not be deported — return to Libya assessed as a breach of his human rights. Released from prison 17 days before the attack.

Prevent Involvement

Referred to Prevent four times. All four referrals were closed — some due to imprisonment, others due to assessments that weighed mental health over extremist ideology.

The Attack

At approximately 7pm, Saadallah attacked two groups of people socialising in Forbury Gardens park. He stabbed six people, killing three: James Furlong (36), Dr David Wails (49), and Joseph Ritchie-Bennett (39). He shouted "Allahu Akbar" and told police afterwards that he "was going to paradise for the jihad." An Islamic State flag was found on his phone.

Outcome

Pleaded guilty. Sentenced to a whole-life term.

Inquest (April 2024, Judge Sir Adrian Fulford): three deaths "probably avoidable." "Significantly troubling failings" in intelligence sharing. The Home Office had dealt with Saadallah with "woeful inadequacy."
Case 5 — Leigh-on-Sea, Essex — 15 October 2021

Ali Harbi Ali — MP Sir David Amess Murdered. Case Closed at McDonald's Meeting.

British-Somali Age 25 Channel — exited 2016
Who He Was

British national born in Croydon to a family of Somali refugees. His school, Riddlesdown Collegiate, referred him to Prevent in 2014 over concerns about interest in violent Islamism — acting correctly in doing so. The case was escalated to Channel. His entire Channel intervention consisted of a single meeting over coffee at a McDonald's. The case was closed in 2016 as low risk. A year after being cleared, he had decided to carry out a terrorist attack in Britain. No further referrals or contact occurred.

The Attack

Ali attended Sir David Amess's constituency surgery at Belfairs Methodist Church Hall in Leigh-on-Sea. He stabbed the MP multiple times. Sir David — a Conservative parliamentarian since 1983, knighted for public service — died at the scene. Ali told an onlooker he had done it "for Syria." He cited Amess's voting record on UK airstrikes as his justification.

Outcome

Convicted of murder and preparation of terrorist acts. Sentenced to life with a whole-life order — he will never be released.

Prevent learning review (February 2025, published after the family waived secrecy): "a series of errors." Case "exited too quickly." Single McDonald's meeting deemed insufficient assessment. Lady Julia Amess: "I am very pleased that the failures of Prevent are now in the public domain — we were aware of them but were essentially gagged."
Case 6 — Bournemouth, Dorset — 12 March 2022

Lawangeen Abdulrahimzai — Tom Roberts, 21, Murdered. Double Murderer Admitted as a Child.

Afghan asylum seeker Claimed age 14, actually 19 Prevent referral 2021
Who He Was

Afghan national who, on 31 July 2018 in Serbia, shot dead two Afghan men with a Kalashnikov rifle during an argument. Convicted of double homicide in Serbia in absentia. Fled to Norway, made an asylum claim (rejected December 2019), then illegally entered the UK through the Port of Poole — claiming to be a 14-year-old child. He was actually 19. No age assessment was conducted. He was placed in foster care in Poole, enrolled in secondary school, and removed from that school after being found with a knife. His fingerprints were taken but not checked against Interpol — a check that would have revealed his Serbian murder convictions. That check was only run after Tom Roberts was dead.

Prevent Involvement

Flagged as "susceptible to terrorism" by the Home Office's Prevent anti-terrorism taskgroup in 2021. A review of his referral was begun. Multiple knife-related incidents in 2020 and 2021 were not acted upon in light of his Prevent flag.

The Attack

On the evening of 12 March 2022, Abdulrahimzai fatally stabbed Tom Roberts — a 21-year-old aspiring Royal Marine and DJ — outside a Subway restaurant in Bournemouth after Roberts intervened in an argument over an e-scooter.

Outcome

Convicted of murder. Sentenced to 29 years in prison.

Coroner ruled no full inquest required — "individual errors" but not "systemic failure." The Home Office's internal review of its own role remains classified. Hansard (January 2025): referenced by a minister alongside the Amess case as a Prevent referral kept secret until inquest.
Case 7 — Colleyville, Texas, USA — 15 January 2022

Malik Faisal Akram — Synagogue Hostage Crisis. Two UK Prevent Referrals. MI5 Cleared Him to Travel.

British-Pakistani, Blackburn Age 44 Two Prevent referrals (2016, 2019)
Who He Was

British citizen born in Blackburn, Lancashire, to a family from Pakistan. History of criminal convictions: drug-related assault, violent disorder, driving offences. Banned from Blackburn magistrates' court in September 2001 for threatening staff — including expressing, on 12 September 2001, that he wished a court usher had died in the 9/11 attacks the day before. Radicalised by 2003 according to his own brother. Long-standing mental health issues described as "well known to the Blackburn community."

Prevent and MI5 Involvement

Referred to Prevent in 2016 and again in 2019 over concerns about his anti-Western and conservative Islamic views (The Times). It is unclear whether he engaged with the voluntary programme. Following six months in Pakistan in 2020, he was investigated by MI5 as a potential terrorist threat under a four-week short-lead investigation. By 2021, MI5 had downgraded him to "former subject of interest" — concluding he no longer posed a threat. This assessment allowed him to obtain a UK passport and travel to the United States without being flagged.

The Attack

On 15 January 2022, Akram entered Congregation Beth Israel synagogue in Colleyville, Texas, posing as a homeless man seeking shelter during Saturday morning Sabbath services. He took four hostages, including the rabbi, and demanded the release of Aafia Siddiqui, a Pakistani al-Qaeda-linked prisoner. The standoff lasted over ten hours. He released one hostage after six hours. When the remaining three hostages escaped, FBI SWAT entered. Akram was shot dead. His final words to his brother by phone: "I've come to die."

Outcome

Killed by FBI agents at the scene.

The attack occurred in the United States, but the failures were entirely British: two Prevent referrals not acted upon, an MI5 investigation closed, and a clearance granted that enabled international travel. The radicalisation, the referrals, and the downgrade all happened on British soil.
Case 8 — Southport, Lancashire — 29 July 2024

Axel Rudakubana — Three Girls Murdered. Three Prevent Referrals. All Closed. "Could and Should Have Been Prevented."

British-Rwandan Age 17 Three Prevent referrals — none escalated to Channel
Who He Was

Born in Cardiff in 2006 to Rwandan parents. Diagnosed with Autism Spectrum Disorder. In December 2019, aged 11, arrived at school by taxi, threatened staff and pupils with a hockey stick on which he had written their names, broke a child's wrist, and was found with a knife. Convicted and given a referral order. Was later found to have downloaded an al-Qaeda training manual (by August 2021), to have purchased ricin seeds and three machetes, and to have maintained an obsessive interest in mass killings, genocide, Hitler, and Genghis Khan. Referred to Prevent three times between 2019 and 2021. Each referral was closed. None was escalated to Channel.

The Three Referrals

2019: Following the hockey stick attack. Closed — no escalation. February 2021: School reported social media posts; assessed "not CT/DE relevant." Closed. April 2021: Teacher saw him researching London Bridge attacks on a school computer during class. Assessed as having "no CT/DE concerns." Closed.

The Attack

On 29 July 2024, Rudakubana attended a Taylor Swift-themed dance workshop for young children at The Hart Space, Southport. He murdered three girls: Bebe King (6), Elsie Dot Stancombe (7), and Alice da Silva Aguiar (9). He attempted to murder eight other children, a dance teacher, and a member of the public. He had also synthesised the biological toxin ricin.

Outcome

Pleaded guilty. Sentenced to life imprisonment, minimum 52 years.

Public Inquiry (Sir Adrian Fulford, April 2026, 763 pages): "This terrible event could have been and should have been prevented." Five areas of systematic failure identified. Rudakubana was passed between agencies in an "inappropriate merry-go-round." Head of Prevent resigned. Prevent learning review: "too much weight was placed on the absence of ideology."
Case 9 — Golders Green, London — 29 April 2026

Unnamed Suspect — Two Jewish Men Stabbed. Prevent Referral 2020. Closed. Attack Declared Terrorism.

British-Somali Age 45 Prevent referral 2020 — closed same year
Who He Was

A 45-year-old British national born in Somalia, who arrived in the UK in the early 1990s. Met Police Commissioner Sir Mark Rowley confirmed he had "a history of serious violence and mental health issues." On the morning of the attack, the same suspect is believed to have attended an address in Great Dover Street, SE1, armed with a knife, injuring the occupant before leaving. Police searched for him but failed to locate him before the Golders Green attack hours later.

Prevent Involvement

Referred to Prevent in 2020. The case was closed in the same year. Metropolitan Police statement (30 April 2026): "We can confirm the suspect was known to the Prevent programme and was subject to a Prevent referral in 2020, which was closed in the same year."

The Attack

At approximately 11:16am on 29 April 2026, the suspect ran along Golders Green Road armed with a knife, targeting Jewish members of the public. He stabbed two orthodox Jewish men: Moshe Shine (Nachman Moshe ben Chaya Sarah), 76, and Shloime Rand, 34. He then attempted to stab police officers before being Tasered and arrested. Counter Terrorism Policing London formally declared it a terrorist attack motivated by antisemitism. Both victims survived. The attack came seven months after two Jews were murdered at a Manchester synagogue on Yom Kippur.

Outcome

Arrested on suspicion of attempted murder. In custody as of the date of publication. Name withheld pending investigation.

This is the most recent confirmed Prevent failure at the time of publication. The pattern is identical to the cases that preceded it: referral made, case closed, attack follows. The Prevent Learning Review has not yet been published. It will be.

What the Numbers Actually Tell You

Prevent's defenders will cite the tens of thousands of cases where intervention succeeded — where a vulnerable person was diverted, where Channel support changed a trajectory. Those cases are real. They are also almost entirely unverifiable, because proving a negative — proving that someone who did not commit an attack would have committed one without intervention — is not possible. What is verifiable is the failure record. And the failure record is documented in official reports, by official reviewers, using official evidence.

90%+
Of Prevent referrals are dismissed at initial assessment without specialist intervention. The programme processes enormous volume. It does not reliably identify genuine threat.
Source: Home Office Prevent statistics; ICCT review, November 2025
16%
Of Prevent referrals in 2021–22 were for Islamist concerns — despite 80% of counter-terrorism police live investigations being Islamist in nature
Source: Shawcross Independent Review of Prevent, February 2023; Counter Terrorism Policing

That gap — between the threat profile and the referral profile — is not an accident. It is the product of deliberate institutional design. Prevent was structured, from its earliest iterations, to avoid the appearance of targeting Muslim communities. The result was a programme that, in the Shawcross review's words, demonstrated "cultural timidity" — a programme that processed referrals for white right-wing suspects at a rate disproportionate to the actual threat they posed, while under-referring in the area where the actual body count was accumulating.

In June 2025 it was reported that the Prevent Assessment Framework included a referral basis for "cultural nationalists" — classifying them as vulnerable to "extreme right-wing terrorist ideology." This, in a programme that has overseen at least nine documented failures involving Islamist terrorism or antisemitic violence by individuals with Somali, Iraqi, Pakistani, Libyan, Afghan, and British-Somali backgrounds.

What Official Reviews Have Found

  • Shawcross (2023): "All too often those who commit terrorist acts in this country have been previously referred to Prevent." Prevent showed "cultural timidity" on Islamism. 80% of CT police investigations Islamist; only 16% of Prevent referrals were Islamist.
  • Southport Inquiry (2026): Attack "could have been and should have been prevented." Five areas of systematic failure. Agencies operated an "inappropriate merry-go-round." Head of Prevent resigned.
  • Reading Inquest (2024): Three deaths "probably avoidable." Home Office dealt with the attacker with "woeful inadequacy." "Significantly troubling failings" in intelligence sharing.
  • Fishmongers' Hall Inquest (2021): Prevention of Future Deaths report issued. Gaps in Prevent officer training. No authorisation process for high-risk offenders attending public events.
  • Ali Harbi Ali — Prevent Learning Review (2025): Case "exited too quickly." "A series of errors." Entire Channel intervention: one meeting at McDonald's.
  • Rudakubana — Prevent Learning Review (2025): "Too much weight placed on absence of ideology." Cases closed without escalation despite documented violence and extremist research. Head of Prevent resigned.

Misconceived from the Start

The original failure of Prevent is not operational. It is conceptual. The programme was designed, from its inception, to avoid the political discomfort of stating what the threat actually was. When Tony Blair's government created it in 2003, the framing was deliberately broad: "all forms of extremism," "vulnerabilities," "safeguarding." When the 2015 statutory duty was introduced, the same architecture was preserved. When Shawcross was commissioned to review it in 2021, the programme had been running for eighteen years and had still not resolved whether its primary function was counter-terrorism or social work.

The answer it eventually settled on was both — which is to say, neither. A programme that tries to be a counter-terrorism operation and a mental health pathway and a community cohesion initiative and a safeguarding service will inevitably fail to excel at any of them. And when it fails at counter-terrorism, people die.

The Golders Green attack on 29 April 2026 — the attack that prompted this article — arrived as an almost perfect summary of the programme's entire record. A 45-year-old man with a history of serious violence. Referred to Prevent. Case closed within the year. Six years later, running along a street in north London stabbing Jewish men. The referral process worked as intended. The programme then did what it does: it closed the case and moved on.

"This has been a series of attacks on our Jewish community, particularly in recent weeks, and there is a very deep sense of anxiety, of concern about security, about safety, about identity frankly."

Prime Minister Sir Keir Starmer — Downing Street, 30 April 2026

He is right that there is anxiety. What he did not say — what no prime minister has said, from Blair through to Starmer — is that the Prevent programme, in its current form, has been a structural contribution to that anxiety rather than its answer. Moshe Shine is 76 years old. He is in hospital. His attacker was known to Prevent. The case was closed in 2020. That is the programme's record, in its most recent entry.

The question is not whether Prevent should exist. Some form of early-intervention mechanism is a legitimate tool of counter-terrorism policy. The question is whether this Prevent — the one that closed the cases of Ahmed Hassan, Khairi Saadallah, Ali Harbi Ali, Axel Rudakubana, and the Golders Green suspect — is fit for purpose. The official record says no. The families of the dead already know the answer.
