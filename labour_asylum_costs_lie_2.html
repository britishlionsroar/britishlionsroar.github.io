Is Labour Once Again Using Selective Wording and Selective Memory to Mislead the Taxpayer?

The government claims it has cut overall asylum support costs by nearly £1 billion. The answer to that question is yes. Here is the evidence.

By Steve English  ·  Editor, The British Lions Roar  ·  April 2026
  • The government quoted a hotel saving of ~£900m. The actual audited fall in total Home Office asylum support spending was ~£700m. Those are not the same number.
  • Alternative accommodation — ex-military bases, HMOs, dispersal housing — carries significant costs the headline does not reflect. The NAO confirmed large sites cost £46 million MORE than hotels.
  • RAF Scampton: £60m spent. Zero asylum seekers housed. £48.5m formally recorded as a loss.
  • Local authorities are absorbing hundreds of millions in costs that do not appear in any asylum support headline.
  • The asylum accommodation contracts have tripled in projected cost, from £4.5bn to £15.3bn over ten years.
  • Small boat arrivals in 2025 reached 41,472 — the second highest on record, up 13% on the previous year. The pipeline is not slowing. The saving, such as it is, is not stable.
  • The OBR has warned spending could be £1.4bn higher by 2028/29 if current trends continue.
  • Foreign nationals now make up 12% of the entire British prison population, at an estimated cost of £643m per year — none of which appears in the asylum support headline.

The government's exact words were these:

"The population in asylum hotels has fallen by nearly 20% in the last year and by 45 per cent since the peak under the previous government — helping reduce asylum support costs by nearly £1 billion." — UK Government statement, 2025

Read that sentence carefully. It does not say hotel costs fell by £1 billion. It says asylum support costs fell by nearly £1 billion. That is a claim about the whole system. And that claim does not survive contact with the actual numbers.

Part I

What the Audited Figures Actually Show

£4.7bn Total asylum support spend 2023/24
£4.0bn Total asylum support spend 2024/25
£700m Actual audited saving — not £1 billion

In 2024/25, the Home Office spent £4bn on asylum support — a reduction of 17% from 2023/24, when the Home Office spent £4.7bn. That is a fall of approximately £700 million. Not £1 billion. The government's own headline overstates the saving by around £200 million before we have even looked at what is excluded.

Within that total, the Home Office managed to reduce spending on asylum hotels in 2024/25 by £0.9 billion compared to 2023/24. That hotel reduction is real. But the government presented the hotel saving — £900m — as if it were the system saving. It was not. Other costs rose to partially offset it. The net saving was £700m, not £900m, and not £1 billion.

Part II

They Moved People Out of Hotels — Into Something More Expensive

The government's strategy for reducing hotel use has been to move people into ex-military bases, HMOs, and dispersal accommodation. This was presented as a cheaper solution. The National Audit Office confirmed it was not.

The Home Office originally estimated set-up costs at former RAF bases would be £5 million each. They increased to £49 million for Wethersfield and £27 million for Scampton. The National Audit Office found that large sites could cost £46 million more than using hotels over the same period — due to high set-up and refurbishment costs coupled with the millions wasted on failed plans.

RAF Scampton — the former home of the Dambusters — is the most egregious example. The Home Office has previously admitted that a total of around £60m was spent on the site, of which £48.5m has been recorded as a loss. Not a single asylum seeker was ever housed there. The plan was scrapped. The bill stayed.

And if the project had been allowed to continue, the losses could have increased to £180m over another three years. Meanwhile, if Wethersfield remains open as planned, the NAO projected it would cost taxpayers a further £338m by 2026-27.

None of these capital costs, write-offs, or inflated running costs appear in the asylum support headline the government is asking the public to applaud.

Part III

The Contracts Have Tripled — and the Government Has No Grip on Them

Asylum accommodation contracts, initially estimated to have a total contract cost of £4.5 billion, are now estimated to cost £15.3 billion over ten years. The National Audit Office — Britain's independent public spending watchdog — stated plainly that the Home Office has "few levers to control costs."

Three private companies — Clearsprings Ready Homes, Serco, and Mears — hold these contracts. In May 2025, these three providers were reported to have made £380 million in profit from their accommodation contracts. Clearsprings' contract value has grown tenfold since being signed: from £0.7 billion to £7 billion. The company recorded profits of £90 million in 2024 — up from under £800,000 in 2020.

The taxpayer's bill is expanding. The contractors are enriched. The government is claiming a saving.

Part IV

The Costs the Headline Was Never Designed to Include

Here is where the selective wording becomes most visible. When a government minister says "asylum support costs," the public reasonably hears: what this country spends supporting asylum seekers. That is not what the phrase means in Whitehall accounting.

Council shortfalls above grant reimbursement. Directly reported asylum-related social care spending by local authorities has more than doubled in real terms since 2019-20, rising from £299 million to £744 million in 2024-25 — a 148 per cent increase. The Home Office pays dispersal grants to councils, but those grants do not cover the full cost. The shortfall — housing pressure, social care, education, safeguarding — is absorbed into general council budgets under headings that are never labelled asylum. Kent alone spent £41.6 million on asylum-related social care — almost double the second highest spending local authority.

Long-term contract inflation. The trajectory from £4.5bn to £15.3bn does not appear as an annual cost, so it does not appear in the annual saving calculation. It will appear on future taxpayers' bills.

The prison and detention costs of foreign nationals. From 2018 to 2025, around 193,000 people were detected reaching the UK in small boats. As of December 2025, 10,376 foreign nationals were held in British prisons — 12% of the entire prison population — at an estimated annual cost to the taxpayer of £643 million including prosecution and court costs. That figure has risen by roughly 50% since 2019. None of it appears in any asylum support headline. It sits in Ministry of Justice budgets, dispersed and invisible — another cost the label was never designed to capture.

Part V

The Pipeline Has Not Slowed — The Saving Is Not Stable

41,472 Small boat arrivals in 2025 — 2nd highest on record
+13% Increase on 2024 arrivals
£1.4bn OBR projected additional spend by 2028/29

Even if you accept the government's £700m saving at face value, the structural conditions that would make it permanent do not exist. A total of 41,472 migrants arrived in small boats in 2025 — the second-highest annual figure on record, representing a 13% increase on 2024. The system is not clearing. The backlog is not gone.

The OBR has warned that £1.4 billion extra could be spent by 2028-29 if spending remained at 2024-25 levels. And the Home Affairs Select Committee has already noted that hotel numbers as of June 2025 represented an 8% increase on the numbers accommodated in June 2024 — which, as the committee put it, "casts doubt on the Home Office's assertion they are continuing to reduce reliance on hotel accommodation."

The saving is being eroded in real time. The government is not telling you that either.

Part VI

What This Is

The label "asylum support costs" sounds like a total. It has never been a total. It is a single Home Office budget line that excludes, by convention or by design, a substantial portion of what the British taxpayer actually spends on asylum seekers — spread across local authority budgets, Ministry of Justice accounts, capital programmes, and failed projects written off under different headings.

The government chose to present a partial number using language that implies a complete one. They chose to quote the hotel saving rather than the smaller total saving. They chose not to mention what the alternatives cost, what the contracts now project to cost, what councils are absorbing, or what the prison system is carrying.

That is not an accounting technicality. That is a choice. And it is a choice that has a purpose.
