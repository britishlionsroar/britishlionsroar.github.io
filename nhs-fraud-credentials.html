The Case Against Mass Overseas Recruitment

The Broken Gate: How Britain Imported a Credential Crisis Into Its Own NHS

Fake degrees. Proxy exam sitters. Ghost employers. Rogue sponsors re-licensed within months. The government called it a staffing solution. The evidence calls it something else.

  • 717 nurses suspected of using proxy sitters for UK competency exams — 48 already registered and working in NHS wards when caught
  • Over 350 individuals wrongly added to the NMC register due to verification failures at test centres in India, Nigeria, and Pakistan
  • A pan-India forgery network seized over 100,000 fake nursing, medical, and engineering certificates — potentially 1 million in circulation globally
  • 470+ care sponsor licences revoked; more than 1 in 10 rogue operators subsequently re-licensed — often under new company names with no inspections
  • Nearly 1 million workers arrived under the Skilled Worker visa in its first three years — triple the Home Office's own forecast
  • The same Home Office that ran this system will now facilitate further large-scale immigration from India under Keir Starmer's 2025 UK-India deal
  • Only one compliance officer was available per 1,600 licensed sponsors at peak
Purpose / Mission

This is the fourth article in The British Lions Roar's NHS Migrant Expose series. The previous three articles documented disciplinary disparities, patient safety failures, and the structural costs of mass overseas recruitment. This article completes the picture by examining the credential and sponsorship fraud that has run alongside that recruitment — and by making the case, plainly and with evidence, that paying more to train and retain British nurses would have been cheaper, safer, and more honest than what was actually done.

There is a number the government does not like to say out loud. It is approximately £20,000. That is the rough annual premium — in higher wages, training costs, and domestic bursaries — that it would cost, per nurse, to grow and retain a British nursing workforce rather than recruiting abroad. For two decades, ministers on both sides chose not to pay it. Instead, they built a system that recruited from the poorest countries on earth, called it ethical, and apparently hoped that nobody would look too carefully at what was coming through the gate.

Some of what was coming through the gate was excellent. Many internationally recruited nurses are skilled, committed professionals who have served patients well. That is not in dispute. What is in dispute — what the evidence now makes impossible to ignore — is that the gate itself was broken. The verification system was inadequate. The sponsorship system was rotten. The enforcement was thin to the point of theatre. And the political will to address any of it came only after scandal forced it.

Part One: The Credential Fraud Infrastructure

India — A Million Fake Certificates in Circulation

In December 2025, Kerala Police dismantled what investigators described as a pan-India forgery network operating at industrial scale. Eleven suspects were arrested. Over 100,000 counterfeit academic certificates were seized — nursing qualifications, medical degrees, engineering credentials — all linked to approximately 22 universities. The network had printing hubs in Tamil Nadu and distribution points in Bengaluru. Certificates were sold for the equivalent of thousands of pounds each, primarily to people seeking employment abroad: Australia, Canada, the Gulf states, and Europe.

The investigators' estimate of total documents produced goes far beyond what was seized. Authorities believe that more than one million forged certificates from this network alone may now be in circulation globally.

This was not a unique event. It built directly on earlier exposures. Manav Bharti University in Himachal Pradesh was found to have sold approximately 36,000 fake degrees over eleven years — of 41,000 degrees it issued, investigators determined that only around 5,000 were genuine. The Enforcement Directorate continued pursuing the university's promoters into 2026, declaring them fugitive economic offenders. In December 2025, a separate Lucknow operation uncovered a PhD-holder-led ring producing forged marksheets and university seals from over 25 institutions.

1,000,000+ Estimated forged academic certificates from one Indian network alone, per investigators Certificates included nursing, medical, and engineering qualifications. Sold for £2,000–£5,000 each. Distribution confirmed across multiple continents.

These forgeries are not crude productions. Sophisticated operations produce documents that pass visual inspection. The UK's verification systems for overseas-trained nurses rely on primary source verification from the issuing institution, English language testing, and competency examinations. When the issuing institution's own records are corrupt, or when the examinations themselves are cheated, the chain collapses.

Nigeria — Proxy Testers in the NHS

In 2024, the Nursing and Midwifery Council identified widespread suspected fraud at a single test centre in Ibadan, Nigeria: the Yunnik Technologies facility, which administered the Computer-Based Test (CBT) required for UK registration. The detection method was a data anomaly — suspiciously fast completion times indicating that candidates were not the ones sitting the exam.

The NMC identified 717 individuals as likely having used proxy sitters — people paid to take the competency test in their place. Of those 717, 48 were already registered and working in NHS roles when the fraud was uncovered. A further 669 were applicants, many working as healthcare assistants or in care homes while pursuing full registration. Nearly 2,000 tests from this centre were invalidated as a precaution, requiring retakes or removal from the register.

48 Already-registered nurses working in the NHS when proxy fraud was identified These individuals were in patient-facing roles — administering medications, providing hands-on care — while under active fraud investigation. At least 10 were subsequently removed from the register.

This is not a small number. Forty-eight people in clinical NHS roles, holding registration obtained through fraudulent means, working with patients. The question that should be asked — and has not been sufficiently answered — is how many others at how many other test centres have not yet been caught.

Pakistan — Diploma Mills and Historical Exposure

The Axact company, headquartered in Karachi, was exposed between 2015 and 2018 as one of the largest diploma mill operations in the world. It sold thousands of fake online degrees to applicants globally, including in the United Kingdom. Some of those degrees were used to obtain or support NHS employment. The British connection was documented at the time and prompted isolated investigations, though no large-scale UK-specific catch was ever announced.

More recently, UK-facing document fraud from Pakistan has shifted toward sponsorship paperwork rather than academic credentials — fake employment letters, fabricated bank statements, and fraudulent visa applications. One 2025 investigation found agents charging up to £50,000 for falsified application packages, some of which were approved despite obvious errors.

Part Two: The NMC's Own Record on Fraudulent Entry

The NMC register is not a passive database. It is supposed to be an active guarantee — a public declaration that everyone on it has been assessed, verified, and found competent. The data from its own reports tells a different story.

Between 2017 and 2021, 101 nurses and midwives were removed from the NMC register specifically for deliberately misleading the regulator about their qualifications — fraudulent entry, in the NMC's own language. A further 144 were removed for incorrect registration, not all of which involved deliberate fraud.

In 2025, the NMC acknowledged that over 350 individuals had been wrongly added to the register due to verification lapses — fraudulent or underqualified applicants approved through inadequate checking of test centres in India, Nigeria, and Pakistan. The breakdown: 16 removals from one Pakistani centre, 19 from a Nigerian centre, and 68 Indian cases where evidence was deemed insufficient for a fraud finding despite concerns.

The Professional Standards Authority, which oversees the NMC, described the period in its review as a "dark period" in the regulator's operation. The NMC's own annual report acknowledged cultural and operational failings in its fitness-to-practise function.

"The NMC's fitness-to-practise function was described by its own oversight body as being in a 'dark period.' This is the regulator of the people caring for you in hospital."

In 2023, a Times investigation found 22 doctors on the GMC register who held overseas bans or sanctions that had not been properly flagged or shared with UK regulators. These were not fraudulent credentials — these were genuine doctors with genuine qualifications who had been found unfit to practise elsewhere and were nonetheless working in the UK. That is a different failure, but it points to the same systemic gap: the verification system is not comprehensive, and gaps are discovered reactively, not proactively.

Part Three: The Sponsorship Scandal

Credential fraud was not the only vulnerability created by mass overseas recruitment. The sponsorship system — the mechanism by which employers bring overseas workers into the UK — became, in the words of investigators and experts, a market for exploitation.

The Gold Rush After 2022

When care workers were added to the Shortage Occupation List in 2022, the number of licensed sponsors exploded. From approximately 32,000 licensed employers in 2021, the total grew to over 123,000 by late 2025. The Home Office, which approved these licences, had roughly one compliance officer for every 1,600 employers. The predictable result followed.

Former Independent Chief Inspector of Borders and Immigration David Neal documented the consequences in a 2024 report. A single bogus firm — referred to as "Company B" in the report — used forged documents in the name of a real care provider to obtain Home Office approval, then received 275 Certificates of Sponsorship for workers to fill roles that did not exist. The firm's address had no trace of a functioning care operation. A separate case involved 1,234 Certificates of Sponsorship issued to a company that declared only four employees.

Between July 2022 and December 2024, the Home Office revoked more than 470 care sector sponsor licences. Those revocations affected approximately 39,000 migrant workers who had already been sponsored and arrived in the UK. Many of them were exploited — underpaid, assigned to non-existent roles, or left stranded when licences were pulled. These were victims of the system. But the system should not have existed in the form it did.

39,000 Migrant workers affected by revoked care sector sponsor licences (2022–2024) Over a quarter of all care workers who arrived during this period were sponsored by employers who subsequently lost their licences. Many faced job loss, exploitation, or were given 60 days to find new sponsorship before facing deportation.

Rogue Operators Recycled Through the System

A February 2026 Times investigation, based on analysis of official records, found that of 566 care firms stripped of their licences between June 2022 and June 2025, 74 — more than one in ten — had subsequently been granted fresh licences to sponsor migrant workers. In many cases, directors of sanctioned firms simply registered new companies that then received approval, sometimes within months, with no inspection, no filed accounts, and spelling variations in the company name apparently sufficient to defeat checks.

One example: Sheena Miles Care Services had its licence revoked after sponsoring 315 visas and being found in breach of Home Office rules. Its sole director, Clarence Masara, then set up Blue Mile Care Ltd. The new company was registered with the Care Quality Commission just three months before receiving a fresh sponsor licence. It had never been inspected. It had filed no accounts. It received the licence anyway.

Professor Brian Bell, the government's own senior immigration adviser, said he was "amazed" that firms were being re-licensed and that temporary bans represented "no punishment at all." He called for minimum five-year bans, up to ten years for serious breaches. Former chief inspector John Vine said the situation "doesn't sound like common sense."

The Fake Job Market

Times journalists joined 13 Facebook groups, with a combined membership approaching one million, where visa sponsorship was openly traded. Over four months, they documented 250 examples of fraudulent sponsorship offers. Agents charged up to £13,000 for what they openly described as "payroll-only" arrangements — the migrant is registered as employed, receives a salary on paper, pays the money back to the agent, and never attends a job. One agent, filmed undercover, confirmed the arrangement explicitly: "On paper you will be working with that company but in reality you won't."

A parallel investigation exposed the Muhammad Munir case, in which a Birmingham-based man posing as a registered accountant and immigration adviser ran a ring that manufactured false employment records for spousal visa applicants. Clients paid their real wages as cash, which was cycled through shell companies and returned as apparent salary from multiple employers — pushing them above the income threshold for a spousal visa. HMRC filings were submitted to make the fiction credible. Munir was sentenced to five and a half years in December 2025.

Part Four: Individual Cases and What They Represent

Statistics describe scale. Individual cases describe consequence.

Zholia Alemi practised as a psychiatrist in the NHS for over twenty years. Her medical degree from New Zealand was forged. She was convicted in 2023 on thirteen fraud counts and sentenced to seven years in prison. In 2025, she was ordered to repay over £400,000 to the NHS. She had earned more than £1 million from the fraud during her time in clinical practice. The GMC's response — a review of approximately 3,000 older Commonwealth-era registrations — found no widespread repeat of this specific failure, but the case exposed that the checks which should have caught her simply did not exist when she registered.

Tanya Nasir forged nine documents, including medical certificates, to obtain a senior nursing position in a neonatal unit in Bridgend, Wales. She was working with premature and critically ill newborns. She was convicted in 2024, sentenced to five years, and subsequently struck off by the NMC.

Oluwabunmu Adeleiyi was a Nigerian national on a student visa who used a false identity — a photograph simply taped onto an existing ID card — to work as a healthcare support worker in NHS facilities in Wales, billing approximately £16,000 per month despite having no medical qualifications. She was caught when a receptionist noticed the doctored passport photo.

Each of these individuals was caught. The question — always the question — is how many were not.

Part Five: The Arithmetic That Was Never Done

Since 2018, the NHS has recruited overseas nurses and midwives at a scale unprecedented in its history. By 2023–24, nearly half of all new NMC registrants — 49.4% — were educated outside the UK. Three countries — India, the Philippines, and Nigeria — account for 80 to 90% of all non-European international recruits. Nigeria and Ghana are on the World Health Organization's red list: countries that cannot be actively targeted for recruitment because removing their healthcare workers damages their own systems. The UK has nonetheless seen tens of thousands of registrations from red-list countries, driven by individual applications that fall outside the active-recruitment prohibition.

The overseas recruitment model was justified, consistently, on cost grounds. Training a nurse in the UK is expensive. Recruiting one already trained abroad is cheaper. On the face of it, that is true: training a British nurse costs the government and taxpayer upwards of £20,000 in direct support, bursary contributions, and clinical placement funding. Recruiting an already-qualified nurse from overseas costs a trust roughly £8,000 to £12,000 in visa, agency, and relocation fees. The saving is real. It was also always incomplete.

It excluded the cost of credential verification failures. It excluded the cost of proxy exam fraud and the regulatory machinery required to investigate it. It excluded the cost of prosecutions, regulatory hearings, and patient safety incidents attributable to unqualified practitioners. It excluded the cost of sponsorship fraud — investigations, enforcement, legal proceedings — and the human cost to the 39,000 workers stranded by revoked licences. And it excluded the most glaring figure of all, which sat in plain sight throughout: the NHS translation budget.

In the 2024/25 financial year, the NHS spent £64 million on translation and interpretation services — confirmed by Freedom of Information data covering 167 trusts and Integrated Care Boards, and reported by The Telegraph in September 2025. The TaxPayers' Alliance's three-year FOI analysis produced a full-system estimate of approximately £174 million. Using the more conservative figure, the NHS was spending over £130,000 every single day so that patients living in Britain could be spoken to in languages other than English.

£64,000,000 NHS annual translation and interpretation spend — 2024/25 (FOI-confirmed, 167 trusts) At the £20,000 training premium per British nurse, this budget alone funds 3,200 British nursing graduates through training. At full Band 5 employment cost (approximately £41,500 including employer contributions), it employs over 1,500 British nurses' full salaries for a year. The TaxPayers' Alliance full-system estimate is £174 million — enough to fund 8,700 British nurses through training.

At the £20,000 premium it supposedly could not afford to pay per British nurse — the exact gap it used to justify mass overseas recruitment — £100 million bridges the deficit for 5,000 British nursing graduates. There are not 5,000 surplus graduates unable to find work. The translation budget alone would solve the entire domestic employment crisis, with money left over.

This matters because there are British nursing graduates who cannot find jobs. In Wales, the Royal College of Nursing reported in April 2026 that up to 50% of nursing graduates were facing unemployment, with recruitment freezes leaving newly qualified nurses on waiting lists or redirected into healthcare support worker roles beneath their qualification level. One cohort of students at Swansea Bay wrote an open letter to their health board. It contained four words that should have stopped every health secretary in their tracks:

"How have we been able to train for jobs that don't exist?"

Wales is the sharpest example but not a unique one. Across England, Band 5 freezes, waiting lists, and graduates leaving the profession entirely — some taking jobs in coffee shops while their registration sits unused — have been documented by the RCN and nursing unions as a national workforce planning crisis.

These graduates were trained in the UK. Their credentials require no verification apparatus. They sat their examinations in person, in English, in institutions this country can audit. They speak the language of their patients without the need for an interpreter. They will not require a sponsorship licence, a Certificate of Sponsorship, or a compliance officer to oversee their employer. They are, in every measurable way, the lower-risk, lower-fraud-exposure option.

The government told them there was no money to employ them. It was spending their salary equivalent — every single year — on translation services for patients who had not learned the language of the country whose health system they were using.

You cannot simultaneously claim that you cannot afford to bridge the training gap for British graduates, while spending the exact sum required to do so on interpreter services. The arithmetic is not complicated. The priorities are simply wrong.

Part Six: The Starmer Deal and the Unanswered Question

In May 2025, the UK and Indian governments announced a bilateral trade agreement. In July, key elements were formalised, including provisions for temporary skilled worker mobility. The government's position is that the agreement does not constitute a mass immigration policy and does not fundamentally change existing visa routes for Indian nationals.

The Kerala certificate forgery network — which investigators believe produced in excess of one million fake credentials, including nursing and medical qualifications — was exposed in December 2025. The raids came after the agreement was signed. On one reading, that means the government could not have known the precise scale of this specific operation when negotiating. On another reading, the scale of credential fraud emanating from India has been documented for years. The Manav Bharti University scandal alone — 36,000 fake degrees over eleven years, exposed in a major SIT investigation — was publicly known long before any bilateral deal was discussed.

The question is not whether Starmer knew about the Kerala raids. He did not. The question is whether a government entering a mobility agreement with India, in a context where credential fraud from India is demonstrably occurring at scale, has taken adequate steps to verify that workers arriving under that agreement will be properly checked. There is no public evidence that enhanced verification mechanisms were included in the deal. The Home Office declined to say how many of the Indian nurse cases it investigated resulted in criminal prosecution. The existing system has already been described by its own oversight body as having been through a "dark period." It is not obvious why a larger flow of workers through the same system will produce better outcomes.

Conclusion: The Gate Was Always the Point

The case for mass overseas NHS recruitment was always primarily political rather than financial. It avoided the confrontation with nursing unions and the Treasury that a serious domestic training expansion would have required. It produced visible, immediately deployable workers rather than the eight-year pipeline that training British nurses from scratch requires. It was presented as pragmatism. What it actually was, in substantial part, was a postponement of decisions that have now become unavoidable at much greater cost.

A broken gate is not a gate. A verification system that allows proxy sitters, forged credentials, and ghost employers is not a verification system. A sponsorship regime in which more than one in ten revoked operators return under new names is not a compliance regime. These are not edge cases in an otherwise functional system. They are structural features of a system that was never properly resourced or designed, because the political incentive was to maximise intake, not to guarantee quality.

The £20,000 per nurse that was never spent on domestic training and retention is now being spent — distributed across fraud investigations, legal proceedings, regulatory overhaul, and the very real costs to patients who encountered the consequences of a gate that was broken before anyone admitted it.

Reform UK's position — domestic workforce first, overseas recruitment as a supplement rather than a foundation, and rigorous enforcement at every stage — is not the hard option. After two decades of the alternative, it is the obvious one.
