For the Sake of the World,
Britain’s Censorship Regime Must End

A newly published draft Free Speech Bill would not merely trim a few excesses from Britain’s speech laws. It would tear up the modern censorship model at the roots — from non-crime speech monitoring to “grossly offensive” communications law to the Online Safety regime itself.

By Special Correspondent · April 2026 · Sources: Draft Bill · Adam Smith Institute · legislation.gov.uk

Britain is constantly told that free speech still exists, just with “reasonable limits”. The bill published through the Adam Smith Institute asks a ruder question: what if those limits have multiplied so far and so vaguely that the right itself has become conditional, selective, and chilled into near-nothing for ordinary people?

Part I

The Bill That Calls Britain’s Bluff

For years, Britain has played a rhetorical game with free speech. Politicians insist the right is secure. Courts gesture toward Article 10. Regulators claim balance. Police forces say they are only maintaining order. Yet the practical message to the public has been rather different: speak carefully, joke carefully, post carefully, complain carefully, and above all do not stray into territory that is politically inconvenient, emotionally abrasive, or bureaucratically disapproved.

The draft Free Speech Bill published through the Adam Smith Institute is significant because it does not pretend this is merely a matter of over-enforcement or a few bad decisions. It is drafted on a much harsher diagnosis. The problem, in its view, is structural. Britain has built layer upon layer of statutes, codes, offences, professional rules and quasi-police practices that let the state, or state-adjacent actors, punish lawful expression indirectly even where a criminal conviction would never stand.

“There is no right in law not to be offended by the expression of others.”

That one sentence, sitting in clause 5, is not decorative. It is the bill’s centre of gravity. It is there to reverse the moral logic of modern British speech regulation. The current regime often behaves as though offence itself is a form of actionable social harm. This bill says the opposite. It says offence, shock, insult and even hatred are not, by themselves, enough. If expression is to be punished, the state must show something much narrower and much more concrete.

Part II

What It Would Actually Change

This is not a single-clause protest bill or a symbolic motion designed to generate headlines and then disappear. It is a full legal framework. It defines lawful expression broadly, protects speech on politics, morality, philosophy, religion and public controversy, restricts the state’s ability to interfere with that expression, and narrows unprotected speech to recognised categories such as direct incitement, unlawful threats, fraud, blackmail, serious harassment, perjury, contempt and national security offences.

Just as importantly, it tries to block the workarounds. Clause 13 prohibits direct and indirect censorship by public authorities. Clause 14 targets non-crime speech monitoring specifically, banning the creation or sharing of records designed to punish or chill lawful expression. Clause 15 says the state cannot outsource censorship to third parties or fund programmes whose purpose is to suppress lawful speech. Clause 16 bars compelled ideological speech as a condition of public employment, education, licensing or benefits.

What survives under the bill

  • Unlawful threats, direct incitement, fraud, blackmail and conspiracy remain punishable.
  • Defamation survives, though it must be read consistently with the new free-expression framework.
  • Harassment remains unlawful, but the bill tightens it so that people are not criminalised merely because others choose to follow or monitor their posts.
  • National security offences, perjury and contempt of court are preserved.

That matters because critics will inevitably pretend the proposal creates a legal free-for-all. It does not. It is not a licence to threaten, stalk, extort, defraud or organise violence. What it does is remove the soft-focus category of punishable expression — the British habit of policing language because someone somewhere says it was alarming, upsetting, grossly offensive or socially unwelcome.

Part III

The Repeal Schedule Is the Real Story

The most explosive section of the bill is Schedule 1. That is where the authors stop speaking in abstractions and start naming names. The repeal list includes section 127 of the Communications Act 2003, the Malicious Communications Act 1988, the Online Safety Act 2023, the Public Order Act 1986, the Public Order Act 2023, the Obscene Publications Act 1959, parts of the Terrorism Acts, and the Hate Crime and Public Order (Scotland) Act 2021.

That is not modest reform. That is demolition. It would mean Britain abandoning decades of accumulated speech-control law and replacing it with a far more speech-protective baseline. If enacted in anything close to this form, the effect would be constitutional in practice even if not in formal label. Britain does not have a written First Amendment. This is an attempt to draft one by statute.

The bill does not merely complain that modern speech law is being misused. It assumes the architecture itself is the problem.

That is why the proposal is interesting even where one disagrees with parts of it. Most British free-speech discussion still operates within the present settlement and asks for nicer enforcement. This bill rejects the settlement. It treats the speech regime built from the late twentieth century onward as a bipartisan legal construction that must be rolled back, not gently supervised.

Part IV

The End of the Non-Crime Speech State

Clause 14 deserves special attention because it strikes at one of the most revealing habits of the modern British state: the urge to log, flag, catalogue and quietly stigmatise lawful expression even when no criminal threshold has been crossed. The bill would prohibit the creation or sharing of such records where the purpose is to punish or chill lawful speech. It would also require records created in breach of that rule to be destroyed.

That matters for a simple reason. A state does not need to prosecute everyone in order to control the boundaries of speech. It only needs to make people understand that they may be recorded, visited, warned, reported to employers, or marked down as troublesome. Once that lesson lands, formal punishment becomes almost optional. The chilling effect does the work on its own.

Britain has become highly sophisticated at this softer form of control. One of the bill’s strengths is that it recognises censorship is no longer only a matter of criminal conviction. It can be administrative, reputational, professional, regulatory and anticipatory. It can arrive through databases, codes of conduct, licensing conditions and state-funded pressure networks rather than a policeman reading out a charge.

Part V

Why This Matters Beyond Britain

The Adam Smith Institute’s argument is not confined to domestic liberty. The authors say Britain’s regulatory model is increasingly outward-facing, particularly through online regulation. That matters because once a country with Britain’s legal and diplomatic reach begins treating vague emotional offence as a regulatory category, the pressure does not stay inside one island. Platforms adapt. Compliance departments globalise. Foreign users are drawn into standards they never voted for and may not even recognise as legitimate.

That is why the line “for the sake of the world” is not merely dramatic dressing. The fear here is that Britain’s censorship habits are exportable: a set of principles in which speech is tolerated only until regulators, politically motivated complainants, or institutional risk managers decide it has become too difficult. In that model, freedom exists on paper and shrinks in practice.

The bill’s wider ambition

  • Protect expression on politics, morality, religion and public controversy.
  • Prevent public authorities from suppressing lawful speech either directly or by indirect pressure.
  • Stop state-funded or state-coordinated censorship by proxy.
  • Give claimants a civil route to fight back and recover costs when their speech rights are breached.
Part VI

Where the Real Argument Will Be

The strongest objection to the bill will not be procedural. It will be moral. Its opponents will say Britain is a more diverse, more combustible, more digitally unstable country than it once was, and that the state therefore needs broad tools to suppress speech before speech becomes disorder. In other words, they will claim peace requires pre-emptive control.

That is a serious argument. It is also the argument that has driven Britain into its present dead end. Give the state broad powers to police “harmful” or “grossly offensive” expression and those powers do not remain narrowly confined. They spread. They sink into institutions. They become employment rules, platform pressures, police databases, regulator guidance and cultural intimidation. In time, everyone is told they are still free. They are simply free only inside a maze.

The bill answers that by forcing the state back onto harder ground. Show the threat. Show the incitement. Show the targeted harassment. Show the actual unlawful act. But do not treat mere offence, disgust, ideological dissent or public controversy as though they were crimes in waiting.

A country does not remain free because ministers say the right words about liberty. It remains free when ordinary people can speak plainly without being processed by a bureaucracy of emotional management.
Part VII

Britain Now Has a Choice

Whatever happens to this draft in Parliament, its publication matters. It drags the argument into the open. Britain can continue down the current road — ever more regulation, ever wider definitions of punishable speech, ever more administrative monitoring, ever more dependence on regulators and institutional gatekeepers. Or it can admit that a free country cannot be built around the legal enforcement of managed inoffensiveness.

This bill is not timid. It is not meant to be. It is a direct challenge to the speech-control consensus that has thickened over decades under governments of different labels. In that sense it does something unusual in modern Britain: it states the scale of the problem honestly.

The real question is not whether every clause survives scrutiny. Some will not. The real question is whether Britain still wants to be a country in which free speech is treated as a liberty first and a bureaucratic risk last — or whether it prefers the opposite and simply lacks the honesty to say so.

That argument can no longer be avoided. The draft bill has made sure of that.

Key clauses

  • Clause 5: no legal right not to be offended.
  • Clause 6: special protection for public-interest speech.
  • Clause 14: non-crime speech monitoring prohibited.
  • Clause 16: no compelled ideological speech for jobs, licences or benefits.
  • Clause 22: anti-SLAPP strike-out procedure.
  • Clause 24: past speech convictions could be set aside where the conduct would now be lawful.

Why it stands out

  • It is drafted as a system reset, not a symbolic protest.
  • It blocks censorship by proxy as well as formal state censorship.
  • It protects the right not to mouth official or ideological formulas.
  • It treats vague offence-based criminalisation as the core problem, not an accidental side-effect.
