Twice as Likely.
The Foreign Nurse Disciplinary Scandal
the NMC Is Trying to Make Disappear.

Internationally educated nurses are referred to disciplinary hearings at roughly double the rate of British-trained nurses. The regulator's response is not to investigate why. It is to pressure hospitals into referring fewer of them — and call it equality.
The British Lions Roar  |  Editor: Steve English  |  April 2026  |  NHS / Regulation / Patient Safety

In Part 1 of this series, we showed how the NHS spends £100 million a year on translation services for non-English-speaking patients while turning away thousands of newly qualified British nurses. The same recruitment pipeline that created that crisis has a second chapter — one the regulator responsible for nursing standards is quietly working to bury.

The Nursing and Midwifery Council is the body responsible for regulating everyone on the nursing and midwifery register in the UK. When a nurse's fitness to practise is called into question — through misconduct, incompetence, communication failures, or patient safety concerns — it is the NMC that investigates, prosecutes, and where necessary suspends or strikes them off. It is, in short, the body that stands between the public and unsafe nursing care.

For years, the NMC's own data and its own commissioned research have shown the same uncomfortable pattern: internationally educated nurses — the same overseas recruits brought in at such scale that 93% of the government's "50,000 more nurses" pledge was filled by foreign recruitment — appear in the fitness-to-practise process at disproportionately high rates. Not by a small margin. By roughly double.

2× Africa-trained nurses were referred to NMC fitness-to-practise processes at more than double the rate their workforce share would predict — 4.4% of referrals from 1.9% of the register. Source: NMC-commissioned University of Greenwich research, published 2017, covering 2012–2014 data.

The research was not conducted by critics or opponents of the NHS. It was commissioned by the NMC itself, carried out by the University of Greenwich, and published on the NMC's own website in 2017. The finding was explicit: nurses trained in Africa faced a referral rate more than double what their share of the register would predict. They were also more likely to progress through the full investigation and hearing stages once referred.

The consequences for those who reached tribunal without legal representation were severe. Of unrepresented internationally educated nurses who appeared before a panel, 83% were suspended or struck off. For those who had legal representation, that figure was 55%. Only 8% of unrepresented overseas nurses received a finding of no impairment — compared to 27% of those who were represented. The system was not designed to help them navigate it.

The current picture is consistent with this pattern. The NMC's 2024/25 fitness-to-practise figures show 6,539 new concerns received — up 13% on the previous year, and running 21% above the NMC's own planned assumptions. Referrals from employers have been rising month on month. The NMC's own council papers describe the volume as "the new normal." Internationally educated nurses and midwives now make up approximately 24% of the register — around 210,000 professionals. Black professionals make up roughly 13% of the register and are consistently over-represented in employer referrals relative to that share.

The NMC does not currently publish a clean breakdown of referrals by country of training in its headline annual statistics — it has moved to reporting by ethnicity instead, which partially obscures the overseas qualification picture. But the pattern the 2017 research identified has not been shown to have reversed. The NMC's own ongoing Ambitious for Change programme acknowledges the disproportionality openly. It simply disagrees with what should be done about it.

"When the data shows one group appearing at higher rates, the institutional response is to investigate less rather than ask why. We have seen this before. It does not end well."

Because the NMC's response to a decade of its own disproportionality data is not to tighten overseas recruitment standards, strengthen induction requirements, or investigate whether training quality from certain countries produces higher patient safety risk. Its response is to reduce the number of internationally educated nurses entering the disciplinary process in the first place — and to do so by pressuring the hospitals and employers who refer them.

In June 2025, the NMC published a set of formal equality, diversity and inclusion targets. The headline commitment: eliminate the "disproportionate pattern of fitness-to-practise complaints received from employers in relation to ethnicity" by 2030. The mechanism for achieving this is not better training, better induction, or better support for overseas nurses adjusting to UK clinical standards. It is a set of tools designed to discourage hospitals from making referrals in the first place.

The NMC's Methods for Reducing "Disproportionate" Referrals — All Currently Active or Launching 2026
  • Mandatory pre-referral advice line. Employers are directed to call the NMC's Employer Link Service before making any referral. Regional NMC advisers help employers "manage concerns locally" instead of escalating to fitness-to-practise. Analysis showed more than half of employer referrals that closed early had not used this line — the implication being they should have been diverted before reaching the process at all.
  • Anti-referral checklists and infographics. Employers receive NMC-produced guidance explicitly titled around "avoiding unnecessary referrals," with lists of steps they must complete before they are permitted to refer. The explicit message: many concerns "would have been better managed locally."
  • Employer bias surveys. The NMC surveys hospitals on what "anti-bias measures" they have in place and shares "best practice" templates encouraging them to think twice before referring ethnic-minority or internationally educated staff.
  • Bias checks at intake. New internal steps flag and review referrals that appear "disproportionate" by ethnicity before they enter the investigation process.
  • Diversified panels. BAME representation on fitness-to-practise panels has been increased to 24% of lay members and 23% of registrant members, with further changes rolling out in 2026.
  • EDI training for all FtP staff. Mandatory equality, diversity and inclusion training specifically focused on how caseworkers handle evidence from overseas-qualified nurses.

To be clear about what this means in practice: a hospital nurse manager who has concerns about an internationally educated colleague's competence, communication, or patient safety record is now expected to call the NMC for pre-referral advice, work through a checklist designed to discourage referral, and demonstrate awareness of anti-bias guidance before they can put a concern into the system. The NMC describes this as improving "referral quality." The practical effect is that fewer overseas-educated nurses end up in disciplinary proceedings — regardless of whether the concerns that prompted the referral were valid.

The NMC's own Ambitious for Change research, produced by the University of Greenwich and examining 288 completed cases from 2019 to 2023, found no evidence of bias in final tribunal decisions. The disparities were found earlier in the process — in how cases were handled before they reached a panel. The NMC's conclusion from this was that the system needed de-biasing at the intake stage. An alternative conclusion — that the higher referral rates for certain groups might reflect real and addressable differences in clinical performance, communication, or adaptation to UK standards — does not appear to have been seriously pursued.

Two Possible Responses to Disproportionate Referral Data
Option A — Address Root Causes
Investigate whether training standards, language proficiency, or adaptation to UK clinical practice are driving higher referral rates for certain groups
Tighten entry standards, strengthen induction, improve support — protect patients while improving outcomes for overseas nurses
Option B — Reduce the Numbers (The NMC's Chosen Approach)
Pressure employers to refer fewer internationally educated nurses, introduce bias checks at intake, mandate pre-referral advice calls
Referral numbers fall. Disproportionality gap closes on paper. Underlying performance or safety concerns remain unaddressed.

This matters because the NMC's remit is patient safety, not equity statistics. The fitness-to-practise process exists because patients were harmed, or were at risk of being harmed, by a nurse whose practice fell below the required standard. Reducing the number of referrals from employers — the people closest to the clinical reality — is not a patient safety improvement. It is an administrative one.

Common themes in fitness-to-practise cases involving internationally educated nurses, as identified in NMC and academic research, include communication failures, record-keeping problems, and difficulties adapting to UK clinical guidelines and professional standards. These are exactly the issues raised in Part 1 of this series — the same communication and language gaps that cost the NHS £100 million a year in translation services. They are not isolated incidents. They are a systemic consequence of a recruitment model that prioritised volume and cost-saving over training standards and patient safety.

The parallel with stop-and-search is exact and deliberate. When data showed that certain groups were stopped and searched at disproportionately higher rates, the institutional response in many forces was to scale back the practice to close the disparity gap. Critics argued — and subsequent data in some areas appeared to support — that reducing policing activity did not address the underlying conditions that generated the disparity. It simply made the disparity less visible. Knife crime statistics in certain cities tell their own story.

The NMC is following the same logic. The disproportionality data is real, acknowledged, and published on the regulator's own website. The response is not to ask what is driving it. The response is to make it go away — by 2030, on paper, through managed reduction of referrals from the employers best placed to identify risk.

Britons won't die out, but they'll become a minority in their own country, with no control over its future. That process is already visible in NHS data — in the recruitment pipelines, in the disciplinary statistics, and in the regulatory responses designed to manage the optics rather than the substance.

Based on published policies, Reform UK is the only party proposing changes of sufficient scale to plausibly interrupt or reverse current demographic trends.

That is the outcome Labour set in motion from 1997 onwards, under Tony Blair, when mass immigration was deliberately embraced as an economic strategy — and it is the outcome the Conservatives later chose to continue.

The NMC's 2030 target will be met. The referral gap will close. And on the wards, the same questions about communication, competence, and patient safety will continue to be asked — by nurses, by managers, and by patients — just not by the regulator.
